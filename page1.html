Primary Treatment



Primary treatment is the first stage of wastewater treatment and involves the physical removal of large solids and organic matter from the wastewater. This is typically done in large settling tanks, where solids settle out of the wastewater due to gravity. The settled solids, called primary sludge, are then removed from the tanks and treated further. Primary treatment can remove up to 60% of the suspended solids and 30% of the biochemical oxygen demand (BOD) from wastewater.

Here's how primary treatment works:

1.Screening:Wastewater first passes through screens to remove large objects like rags, sticks, and plastics.

2.Grit removal:The wastewater then flows through a grit removal tank, where heavy inorganic materials like sand, gravel, and grit settle out

3.Primary settling:The wastewater then enters large settling tanks, also called primary clarifiers. Here, the wastewater velocity is slowed down, allowing heavier settleable solids to sink to the bottom as sludge. Lighter materials like fats, oils, and greases (FOG) float to the top and are skimmed off.

Primary treatment is an essential step in the wastewater treatment process as it helps to protect the secondary treatment processes from being overloaded with solids. Secondary treatment processes, which typically involve biological treatment, are more sensitive to solids than primary treatment.
