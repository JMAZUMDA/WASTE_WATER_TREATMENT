Secondary Treatment



Secondary treatment is the second stage of wastewater treatment and focuses on biologically removing dissolved organic matter from the wastewater. This stage follows primary treatment, which removes large solids and debris.

The Process:

1.Mixing:Wastewater from primary treatment is mixed with a thick, soupy mixture called activated sludge, which is rich in microorganisms.

2.Feeding Frenzy:The organic matter in the wastewater serves as a food source for the microorganisms in the activated sludge. These microbes break down the complex organic molecules into simpler substances like water and carbon dioxide.

3.Separation:After a period of aeration (providing oxygen to the microbes), the mixture is then transferred to settling tanks. Here, the activated sludge settles out of the treated wastewater, allowing the clarified effluent (treated wastewater) to be separated from the sludge.

2.Sludge Management:The settled activated sludge is then thickened, and a portion is recycled back to the aeration basins to maintain a high concentration of microorganisms. The remaining sludge undergoes further treatment and disposal.

Secondary treatment is highly effective in removing dissolved organic matter from wastewater. It can achieve up to 90% reduction in Biochemical Oxygen Demand (BOD), a measure of the amount of oxygen required by microorganisms to decompose organic matter in water. This significantly reduces the environmental impact of the wastewater discharge.
